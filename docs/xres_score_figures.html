Cross-resolution skill figures — a reader's guide

This page documents the verification figures produced by python run_xres.py --stage compare (module xres/xscores.py, plus the PDF panels in xres/xcombined.py). All of them are computed from the same two ingredients, for each of the 12 extreme events:

  • The forecast ensemble: 24 GenCast members initialized 14 days (week-2) before the event peak, reduced to that event's headline metric — weekly-mean T2m anomaly (K), weekly-mean 850 hPa wind speed (m/s), or weekly-total precipitation (mm) — as a (member, lat, lon) field over CONUS. One ensemble at 0.25°, one at 1.0°.
  • The truth: the same metric computed from ERA5 over the same verification week.

Grid matching — every figure comes in two verification batches:

  • Own-grid batch (no filename suffix): each resolution is verified against ERA5 sampled onto its own grid. The 0.25° ensemble is scored against native 0.25° ERA5 (the grids coincide exactly); the 1.0° ensemble against ERA5 nearest-neighbour sampled onto the 1.0° forecast grid. The 1.0° model is never penalized for sub-degree structure it cannot represent — "how good is each system at its own game".
  • Common-baseline batch (files ending _vs_era5_0p25): BOTH ensembles are verified against the same native 0.25° ERA5. The 1.0° members are nearest-neighbour upsampled onto the 0.25° grid first, so the coarse model IS penalized for the fine-scale structure it cannot produce — "which system better predicts the real (fully resolved) atmosphere". Expect the 1.0° scores to worsen relative to the own-grid batch; the gap between the two batches measures how much of the 0.25° model's advantage comes purely from resolving finer scales. In the PDF figures this variant simply drops the ERA5 1.0° curve and keeps the single dark-red ERA5 0.25° curve as the reference for both forecasts.

Colors and styles (consistent across all figures, chosen to survive color-vision deficiency: hue, lightness, line style and marker all encode the same information):

  • dark red, solid — ERA5 truth, 0.25° grid
  • dark blue, solid — ERA5 truth, 1.0° grid
  • light red, dotted, circle markers — GenCast 0.25° forecast
  • light blue, dotted, triangle markers — GenCast 1.0° forecast

Rule of thumb: dark + solid = observed, light + dotted = forecast; reds = 0.25°, blues = 1.0°. In the score figures (Brier, rank histograms) both curves are forecasts, so the dark family shades are used with solid-red vs dashed-blue lines.

0. The PDF panels (xres_combined_*.png) — what changed

The PDFs are now binned histograms, generated exactly the way TransferLearning-QG does it in FiguresProduction.ipynb (PDF_histogram): all values of the field over CONUS are pooled, counted into 100 equal-width bins spanning mean ± 4σ, and each bin's count is divided by the total sample size N:

PDF(bin b) = count(x ∈ bin b) / N

so the y-axis is the probability of a grid point falling in that bin (the QG repo keeps the division by bin width commented out; we match that). Two deliberate departures, both needed because a panel overlays four curves: the bin range is shared by all curves in a panel (otherwise per-bin probabilities would not be comparable), and the x-axis stays in physical units instead of the repo's x/σ standardization. The y-axis is logarithmic from 10−6 to 1, so tails about five orders of magnitude below the mode remain visible. A curve pinned at the very bottom means "zero counts in this bin" (the floor value 10−6 is only a plotting floor, not data).

Reading tip: the interesting region is the tail on the extreme side (right for heat/wind/rain, left for cold events). A forecast whose dotted curve dies off before the solid truth curve under-forecasts the extreme; one that extends beyond it over-forecasts.

1. Brier score (xres_brier.png) — is the tail captured?

What it is

The Brier score is the mean squared error of a probability forecast for a binary event. Over the G grid points (latitude-weighted, weights wg ∝ cos φ):

BS = Σg wg (pg − og)² / Σg wg ,   0 ≤ BS ≤ 1,  lower is better

where at each grid point pg = fraction of the 24 members exceeding a threshold, and og = 1 if ERA5 exceeded it, else 0.

How the figure is built

  • The "event" is tail exceedance: the threshold is set at the 75th, 90th, 95th and 99th percentile of the ERA5 truth field itself (on the matched grid), taken in the event's extreme direction — for cold events (Uri, Elliott, Polar Vortex) the mirrored low percentile is used and "exceedance" means colder.
  • Each panel (4×3 grid, one per event) shows BS as a function of that threshold percentile, one curve per resolution.
  • The grey dotted reference is the score of a climatological forecast that always issues the base rate f = 1 − p/100: since by construction a fraction f of truth points exceed the threshold, its Brier score is f(1−f). A forecast curve below the grey line has skill at that threshold; above it, you would have been better off forecasting the climatological frequency.
Worked example. Take the 95th-percentile threshold, so f = 0.05: the grey reference is 0.05 × 0.95 = 0.0475. Suppose at some grid point 6 of 24 members exceed the threshold, so p = 0.25. If ERA5 exceeded it there (o = 1), that point contributes (0.25 − 1)² = 0.5625; if not (o = 0), it contributes 0.0625. A sharp, correct ensemble (p ≈ 1 where the event happens, p ≈ 0 elsewhere) drives the weighted average toward 0. An ensemble whose members never reach the threshold scores exactly f — i.e. 0.05 — which is above the 0.0475 reference: completely missing the tail is worse than climatology. That is precisely the "is the tail captured?" question this figure answers at a glance.

How to read it

  • Red below blue at the 95–99th percentiles ⇒ the 0.25° ensemble captures that event's tail better than the 1.0° one (and vice versa).
  • Both curves hugging the grey line at 99% ⇒ neither resolution has real skill for the most extreme exceedances at a 14-day lead — only broad-brush probability.
  • Scores generally decrease toward higher percentiles simply because the event gets rarer (both f(1−f) and BS shrink); always judge skill relative to the grey reference, not by the raw number.

2. CRPS (xres_crps.png) — which resolution is better overall?

What it is

The Continuous Ranked Probability Score generalizes the Brier score from one threshold to all thresholds at once: it is the squared distance between the forecast's cumulative distribution F and the truth's step function,

CRPS(F, y) = ∫ ( F(z) − 1{z ≥ y} )² dz

For a finite M-member ensemble it is computed (via properscoring.crps_ensemble, which uses the standard fair estimator) as

CRPS = (1/M) Σi |xi − y| − (1/2M²) Σi Σj |xi − xj|

where xi are the member values and y the ERA5 value at that grid point. The first term rewards accuracy (members close to truth), the second rewards sharpness (it forgives spread only insofar as it is genuinely needed). CRPS has the same units as the variable (K, m/s, mm) and reduces to the absolute error when the ensemble collapses to a single member — think of it as "the probabilistic MAE". Lower is better.

How the figure is built

  • Per event: CRPS at each grid point, then the latitude-weighted CONUS mean.
  • Bars grouped per event, one bar per resolution — each scored against ERA5 on its own grid (0.25° vs native ERA5; 1.0° vs ERA5 sampled to the 1.0° grid).
  • Events are split into three panels by metric family so units stay homogeneous (you cannot meaningfully compare Kelvin bars to millimetre bars).
  • The percentage above each pair is (CRPS0.25 − CRPS1.0) / CRPS1.0: negative ⇒ 0.25° wins, positive ⇒ 1.0° wins.
Worked example. A 3-member ensemble forecasts {−1, 0, 2} K and the truth is 1 K. First term: (|−1−1| + |0−1| + |2−1|)/3 = (2+1+1)/3 = 1.333. Second term: pairwise gaps |−1−0|, |−1−2|, |0−2| each counted twice plus zero diagonals ⇒ Σ|xi−xj| = 2×(1+3+2) = 12, so 12/(2·3²) = 0.667. CRPS = 1.333 − 0.667 = 0.667 K. A single deterministic forecast of 0 K would score |0−1| = 1 K — the spread-aware ensemble beats it even though no member hit the truth.

How to read it

  • This is the headline "which is better, 0.25° or 1.0°?" figure: count the signs of the percentages within each metric family.
  • Caveat: because each resolution is verified on its own grid, the two bars answer "how good is this system at its own game" — the cleanest apples-to-apples framing, but remember the 0.25° task (resolving sharper truth) is intrinsically harder.

3. Rank histogram (xres_rank_hist.png, xres_rank_hist_pooled.png) — is the ensemble honest?

What it is

CRPS and Brier ask "how close?"; the rank histogram asks "is the ensemble's spread statistically honest?". At each grid point, count how many of the 24 members fall below the ERA5 value — that rank is an integer from 0 (truth below every member) to 24 (truth above every member). If truth were statistically indistinguishable from the members (a perfectly calibrated ensemble), every rank would be equally likely:

P(rank = r) = 1/(M+1) = 1/25 = 0.04  for all r

The histograms pool ranks over all CONUS grid points of an event (per-event panels, 4×3) or over all events (pooled figure); the black dotted line is the flat 1/25 target.

Worked example. Truth is drawn from the same distribution as 24 members ⇒ among the 25 values (truth + members), truth is equally likely to land in any position ⇒ flat histogram. Now suppose the ensemble is too narrow (members hug their mean): the truth frequently falls outside the envelope, so ranks 0 and 24 pile up — a U shape. If the forecast has a warm bias, truth sits below most members, rank 0/low ranks spike — a one-sided ramp. If the ensemble is too wide, truth is almost always somewhere in the middle — a hump.

How to read it

ShapeDiagnosis
Flat at 0.04Well calibrated: spread matches actual error
U shape (both ends high)Under-dispersed / over-confident: real-world outcomes escape the ensemble envelope
Hump in the middleOver-dispersed: ensemble wastes probability on outcomes that never verify
One-sided spike (rank 0 or 24)Systematic bias (e.g. a spike at rank 24 = truth above all members = forecast biased low)
  • Compare red vs blue shapes per panel: a flatter histogram at one resolution means its ensemble spread is more trustworthy for that event — even if its CRPS is worse.
  • Caution: with 24 members and one event, spatial correlation makes grid points far from independent, so per-event histograms are noisy; the pooled figure is the more robust summary.

How the three figures fit together

  • PDF panels: qualitative — do the forecast distributions look like truth, especially in the extreme tail?
  • Brier: quantifies exactly that tail question, threshold by threshold, with a no-skill reference line.
  • CRPS: one number per event over the whole distribution — the cleanest 0.25°-vs-1.0° scoreboard.
  • Rank histogram: whether each ensemble's uncertainty is honest — calibration, which neither CRPS nor Brier isolates on its own.
