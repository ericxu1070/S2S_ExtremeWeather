01

Weather vs. climate

The single most useful distinction to get straight first:

  • Weather is the state of the atmosphere at a moment — is it raining in Denver right now, how hard is the wind blowing, what's the temperature this afternoon. It's chaotic and only predictable a week or two ahead.
  • Climate is the statistics of weather over long periods — the average July temperature in Denver, how often it floods, the distribution of outcomes. Climate is what you expect; weather is what you get.

A common one-liner: "Climate is what you plan for, weather is what you dress for." The reason the distinction matters for machine learning is that different models target different points on this spectrum, and the one in between — weeks to a season ahead — is exactly where this project lives.

🔑 Why the middle ground is hard

Day-ahead weather is driven by the current atmospheric state (an initial-value problem). Decade-ahead climate is driven by slow boundary conditions like CO₂ and ocean heat (a boundary-value problem). The subseasonal-to-seasonal (S2S) range — 2 to 6 weeks — sits in the awkward gap where the initial weather has mostly become unpredictable but the slow climate signals are still weak. That's the frontier this work targets.

02

The atmosphere: a fluid in a thin shell

The atmosphere is a shallow envelope of gas — 99% of its mass is within about 30 km of the surface, on a planet 6,371 km in radius. Proportionally, it's thinner than the skin on an apple. Weather is what happens when the Sun heats this fluid unevenly (equator more than poles, land differently than ocean), and the fluid moves to redistribute that heat while spinning on a rotating planet.

To describe "the state of the atmosphere" at any instant, you need to know a handful of physical quantities everywhere — at every horizontal location and at many heights. Those quantities are the variables of the next section. First, the vertical structure:

👆 Click a layer to learn what happens there. Weather — and most of what ML models predict — lives in the lowest layer, the troposphere.
The vertical structure of the atmosphere (not to scale). Forecasting models resolve the troposphere and lower stratosphere in detail.
Why temperature falls with height, then rises again Physics

In the troposphere, the air is mostly heated from below — sunlight warms the surface, which warms the air in contact with it. Rising air expands into lower pressure and cools (adiabatic cooling), giving the characteristic lapse rate of roughly 6.5 °C per kilometer. In the stratosphere the trend reverses because ozone directly absorbs ultraviolet sunlight, heating that layer from within. This temperature inversion is what makes the stratosphere so stable (little vertical mixing) — which is why airliners cruise there.

03

The core variables

Here is the heart of it. The "state" that every forecast model — physical or ML — reads in and predicts is a small set of physical fields. If you internalize these, most of weather ML notation becomes readable. Each is a number defined at every grid point (and, for the 3-D ones, at every height).

🌡️

Temperature

T · t2m (2 m)

How hot the air is. Reported in kelvin (K) internally. The 2-metre temperature t2m is the "what it feels like outside" number weather apps show.

🎈

Pressure

sp · msl

The weight of air above a point. Lows bring storms, highs bring calm. Mean-sea-level pressure msl is what makes the H's and L's on a weather map.

💨

Wind

u · v · (w)

Air motion, split into eastward (u) and northward (v) components. Combine them for speed & direction. Vertical wind w is small but drives storms.

💧

Humidity

q · r · d2m

Water vapour in the air. Specific humidity q (kg water / kg air) is the model's currency; relative humidity r (%) is what people quote.

🗺️

Geopotential

z

The height of a given pressure level — e.g. how high up you find the 500 hPa surface. The workhorse variable for spotting troughs, ridges & storm tracks aloft.

🌧️

Precipitation

tp

Rain + snow reaching the ground, accumulated over a period (mm). A diagnostic output, not part of the core state — and famously the hardest to get right.

⚠️ Kelvin, Pascals, and "why the numbers look weird"

Scientific datasets store temperature in kelvin (0 K = absolute zero, so water freezes at 273.15 K) and pressure in pascals (so standard sea-level pressure is 101,325 Pa, usually quoted as 1013.25 hectopascals, hPa). When a plot looks like it says the temperature is "295," that's 21.85 °C — a pleasant day, not the surface of the Sun.

Vertical levels: the third dimension

Temperature, wind, humidity and geopotential aren't single numbers — they vary with height. So the atmosphere is sliced into vertical levels, and each variable is stored once per level. There are two common ways to define "height":

Pressure levels

Defined by pressure, not altitude — e.g. the 500 hPa level is wherever pressure equals 500 hPa (~5.5 km up). Convenient because atmospheric dynamics are naturally expressed in pressure coordinates, and it's what most datasets ship. A typical set: 1000, 925, 850, 700, 500, 300, 250, 100, 50 hPa … (13 or 37 levels).

Model levels

The native terrain-following layers a physics model actually computes on (137 levels in ECMWF's model). They hug the ground and stretch out with height. More faithful to the physics, but awkward to compare across models, so results are usually interpolated to pressure levels for sharing.

You'll constantly see variable names with a level attached: t850 = temperature at 850 hPa, z500 = geopotential at 500 hPa, u850 = eastward wind at 850 hPa. A few levels carry outsized meaning:

  • 850 hPa (~1.5 km): just above surface friction — the go-to level for "real" temperature and wind, and for tracking warm/cold air masses. This project scores u850 wind speed.
  • 500 hPa (~5.5 km): the mid-troposphere steering level. z500 troughs and ridges tell you where storms will go — the single most-watched field in meteorology.
  • 250–300 hPa (~10 km): the jet-stream level; fast ribbons of wind that organize weather systems.
How many variables is "the state," really? Numbers

A typical ML weather model's input/output state is roughly: a handful of surface (2-D) variables — say t2m, 10 m u & v wind, mean-sea-level pressure — plus a few upper-air (3-D) variables — geopotential, temperature, u, v, w, specific humidity — each replicated across ~13 pressure levels.

That works out to roughly 6 × 13 + 5 ≈ 83 fields. At 0.25° resolution each field is a 721 × 1440 grid (~1 million numbers). So one snapshot of "the atmosphere" is on the order of 80–90 million numbers — and the model maps one such snapshot to the next. That tensor shape, [variables × levels × lat × lon], is the state vector.

04

Grids & resolution: turning the sky into an array

Computers can't store a continuous field, so the atmosphere is sampled on a grid of points spaced evenly in latitude and longitude. The spacing is the resolution, quoted in degrees. Smaller degrees = finer detail = more grid points = more compute.

Resolution≈ spacingGrid size (global)What it resolves
1.0°~111 km181 × 360Large systems; mountains & coasts blurry. Cheap.
0.25°~28 km721 × 1440The standard for modern ML models (ERA5, GraphCast). Sees fronts, hurricanes' structure.
≤ 3 kmconvection-permittingregional onlyIndividual thunderstorms (e.g. HRRR over the US).
🧮 Why 0.25° matters — and costs

Going from 1.0° to 0.25° is 4× finer in each horizontal direction = 16× more grid points, and the memory/compute balloons accordingly. That's exactly the tension in this project: the 0.25° checkpoint needs ~50 GiB of GPU memory per ensemble member and must run one member at a time, whereas the 1.0° model is small enough to parallelize. Finer resolution buys sharper extremes but is dramatically more expensive.

Latitude, longitude, and the pole problem Detail

A regular lat–lon grid has a nasty property: grid cells shrink toward the poles (all meridians converge). A 0.25° cell is ~28 km wide at the equator but only a few km near the poles, which wastes resolution and can destabilize physics models. Physical models often use clever grids (reduced Gaussian, cubed-sphere, icosahedral) to fix this; many ML models sidestep it with graph or spherical-harmonic representations. GraphCast, for instance, learns on a multi-resolution icosahedral mesh rather than the raw lat–lon grid.

05

Time & the scales of prediction

Just as space is discretized into a grid, time is discretized into time steps. A model reads the state at time t and produces the state at t + Δt. For most ML weather models Δt is 6 or 12 hours. To forecast further out, you feed the output back in as the next input and repeat — this is autoregressive rollout (more in §11).

🔑 The predictability wall

The atmosphere is chaotic: tiny errors in the initial state grow exponentially. Beyond ~2 weeks, you fundamentally cannot predict whether a specific Tuesday will be rainy — this is the "butterfly effect," and no amount of ML repeals it. What you can still predict at S2S range are shifts in the odds: "the week ending on the event peak is likely to run hot." That's why S2S forecasting is inherently probabilistic, and why ensembles (§11) matter so much here.

06

Anomalies & climatology: the language of "unusual"

Saying "it was 30 °C" is meaningless without context — 30 °C is freakish in Alaska in winter and mild in Phoenix in summer. So climate science works in anomalies: the departure from what's normal for that place and time of year.

anomaly = actual value − climatology e.g. a +6 K T2m anomaly = six degrees hotter than the typical value for that location and calendar week

The "normal" baseline is the climatology: a long-term average (often 1991–2020) computed separately for each location and each day/week of the year. Subtracting it removes the boring, predictable seasonal cycle and the fixed geography, leaving just the signal — how unusual today is. This is why extreme-event work is framed in anomalies: a heatwave is a large positive temperature anomaly, by definition.

Anomalies, standardization, and why models like them Method

Beyond subtracting the mean, values are often standardized — divided by the local standard deviation — giving a z-score (units of "sigmas"). A +2σ event is roughly a 1-in-40 occurrence; +3σ is genuinely rare. This makes different variables and locations comparable on one scale.

ML models care because raw fields have wildly different magnitudes (geopotential ~50,000 m²/s², humidity ~0.01 kg/kg). Networks train far better when every input channel is normalized to roughly zero mean and unit variance — which is exactly what per-variable, per-level statistics (the "stats" files this project downloads) are for. Anomaly framing and ML normalization are the same idea wearing different hats.

07

Where the data comes from

To train and evaluate a weather model you need a complete, gap-free record of the atmosphere's state through history. But raw observations (stations, balloons, satellites, buoys) are scattered, irregular, and full of holes. The solution is reanalysis.

Reanalysis: the best guess of history

A reanalysis runs a physics weather model over decades of the past, continually nudging it toward every available observation (a process called data assimilation). The result is a physically consistent, complete grid of every variable, everywhere, every few hours, going back decades. It's the closest thing to "ground truth" for the global atmosphere — even though it's itself a model-observation blend.

⭐ ERA5 — the dataset behind almost everything

ERA5 is ECMWF's flagship reanalysis: hourly, 0.25°, ~137 levels, 1940–present. It is the de facto training data and truth standard for nearly every ML weather model — GraphCast, GenCast, Pangu, FourCastNet all learn from ERA5 and are scored against it. When this project "scores against ERA5 truth," ERA5 is playing the role of reality.

SourceTypeRole here

⚠️ "Truth" is a model too

Reanalysis is the best estimate we have, but it's not perfect — it can smooth out the most extreme peaks (a real 3-km thunderstorm downpour gets averaged over a 28-km cell). This is a live issue for extreme-event work: sometimes the ML forecast, the reanalysis "truth," and the true event all disagree, and part of the science is knowing which to trust. It's why higher-resolution truth like HRRR is valuable when available.

08

Extreme events

Averages are easy; extremes are what hurt people and what forecasts most need to get right. An extreme event is a large excursion into the tail of the distribution — a heatwave, cold snap, hurricane, or extreme-rain episode. In anomaly language, they're the biggest-magnitude anomalies.

🔥

Heat waves

Large sustained positive t2m anomalies over days. Often tied to stalled high-pressure "blocking" ridges.

❄️

Cold-air outbreaks

Arctic air plunging south; large negative t2m anomalies. Linked to a disrupted polar vortex.

🌀

Hurricanes

Intense low-pressure cores with extreme u/v winds. Track & intensity are the forecast targets.

🌊

Extreme rain

Huge tp totals in short windows → flooding. The hardest variable to predict well.

The reason extremes are hard for any model, ML included: by definition there are few examples of them in the training record, they live where the model has seen the least data, and they're often small and short-lived so coarse grids blur them. Evaluating whether an ML forecast captures extremes — not just the calm average — is precisely the question this project is built to answer.

09

The classical way: physics-based forecasting (NWP)

For 70 years, weather has been predicted by Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP): write down the equations of fluid motion and thermodynamics that govern the atmosphere, chop the globe into a grid, and step those equations forward in time on a supercomputer. This is what ECMWF's IFS, the US GFS, and every operational center runs.

The equations being solved Physics

NWP integrates the primitive equations — a form of the Navier–Stokes equations for a rotating, stratified fluid — coupled with thermodynamics and moisture:

  • Momentum (Newton's 2nd law): how winds accelerate under pressure gradients, gravity, and the Coriolis force from Earth's rotation.
  • Continuity: mass is conserved — air doesn't appear or vanish.
  • Thermodynamic energy: how temperature changes with heating, compression, and mixing.
  • Moisture & state: water tracking and the ideal-gas law relating p, ρ, T.

Processes too small to resolve on the grid — cloud microphysics, turbulence, convection — are approximated by parameterizations. These are the largest source of NWP error and the hardest part to tune.

✅ NWP's strengths and ❌ its cost

NWP is physically grounded, generalizes to unseen conditions, and is genuinely trustworthy. But a single high-resolution global forecast can take hours on thousands of supercomputer cores. Running the dozens of parallel forecasts needed for an ensemble is enormously expensive — which is the door ML walked through.

10

What is an emulator?

An emulator is a machine-learning model trained to reproduce the input→output behavior of an expensive physical model, but orders of magnitude faster. Instead of solving the physics equations, it learns the mapping directly from data — "given this atmospheric state, what does the next state look like?" — by training on decades of reanalysis.

🧮 Physics model (NWP)

Solves fundamental equations from first principles every run. Slow, expensive, but grounded in physical law and extrapolates to novel situations.

🧠 ML emulator

Learns the input→output pattern from historical data. Once trained, produces a forecast in seconds on a single GPU — but only as good as the data it learned from.

The headline result of the last few years is that these emulators are not just faster — for many variables at medium range they now match or beat the physics model they learned from, at a tiny fraction of the cost. That's what makes them revolutionary, and what makes it feasible to run large ensembles for S2S extremes on modest hardware.

⚠️ "Emulator" vs. "climate emulator" — a naming caution

The word emulator shows up in two nearby contexts. A weather emulator (GraphCast, GenCast) mimics a short-range forecast model step. A climate emulator usually means a cheap surrogate for a full climate model's long-term response to forcings like CO₂. Same core idea — learn the expensive model's behavior cheaply — different target timescale. This project is squarely in the weather-emulator-pushed-to-S2S camp.

Autoregressive rollout & error growth ML detail

Because the model only learned a single Δt step, long forecasts are made by chaining — the output of step n becomes the input of step n+1. This is powerful but dangerous: small per-step errors compound, and the state can drift into unphysical regions the model never saw in training. Models are trained with multi-step rollouts and careful normalization to stay stable. For a 6-week S2S forecast at a 12-hour step, that's ~84 chained calls — a demanding stability test, and a key reason S2S is hard even for ML.

Deterministic vs. ensemble (and diffusion)

This is the most important conceptual split, and it's why GenCast is special.

🎯 Deterministic

Produces one forecast — a single best guess of the future state. Great for short range. But it can't tell you how confident to be, and it tends to blur out extremes (predicting a safe average is the way to minimize error).

🎲 Ensemble / probabilistic

Produces many forecasts (an ensemble), each a plausible future, together forming a probability distribution. The spread is the uncertainty. Essential at S2S range, where the honest answer is "here are the odds," not "here's the number."

🔑 Why GenCast uses diffusion

GenCast is a diffusion model — the same family of generative AI behind image generators. Instead of predicting one deterministic next state, it starts from random noise and iteratively denoises it into a sample of a plausible next atmospheric state. Run it many times with different noise and you get a physically sharp ensemble — each member realistic (not blurred), and the collection spanning the true range of possibilities. That combination — sharp and well-calibrated — is why it's the tool of choice for probabilistic extreme-event forecasting.

What "24 members" means & the multiple-of-GPU rule This project

An ensemble member is one complete run of the model from the same starting point but with different random noise, giving one possible future. This project rolls 24 members per event; together they estimate the forecast distribution over the verification week.

In the parallelized (1.0°) path, members are spread across visible GPUs with pmap, so the member count must be a multiple of the GPU count. The memory-heavy 0.25° path can't parallelize (one member alone needs ~50 GiB), so it runs members serially — which is why this project's newer method assigns one member per PBS array subjob and caches each to disk.

How we score a forecast

A forecast is only meaningful if you can measure how good it is against what actually happened (the ERA5 truth). Different metrics answer different questions.

MetricForWhat it measures
RMSEDeterministicRoot-mean-square error — average size of the miss, in the variable's units. Lower is better.
ACCDeterministicAnomaly correlation — does the forecast get the pattern of anomalies right (0–1)?
CRPSEnsembleContinuous Ranked Probability Score — the go-to score for a probabilistic forecast. Rewards a distribution that is both accurate and appropriately confident. Lower is better.
Rank histogramEnsembleA calibration check: is the truth equally likely to fall anywhere within the ensemble spread? A flat histogram = well-calibrated.
Brier / ROCProbabilistic eventsSkill at yes/no event forecasts ("will it exceed the heatwave threshold?").
CRPS & the sharpness–calibration trade-off Stats

CRPS generalizes mean-absolute-error to probability distributions. For a forecast CDF F and observed value y, it integrates the squared difference between F and the step function at y. If the forecast collapses to a single value, CRPS reduces exactly to absolute error — so it fairly compares deterministic and ensemble forecasts on one scale.

A good probabilistic forecast balances two virtues: sharpness (confident, narrow distributions) and calibration (the stated probabilities are honest). You can be sharp and wrong (overconfident) or calibrated but useless (always predicting climatology). CRPS penalizes both failure modes at once — which is why it, plus a rank histogram for calibration, is the standard reporting pair for ensemble work like this.

📐 Verification window

This project doesn't score a single instant — it scores the week-mean anomaly over the 7 days ending at the event peak, weeks ahead of that peak. That's the honest S2S question: not "will it be hot next Tuesday" but "does the forecast, issued weeks early, put the odds on an unusually hot verification week?"
