01

The task: turning coarse weather into fine weather

Two weather datasets sit at opposite ends of a resolution gap, and the model's job is to bridge it.

ERA5 is a global reanalysis on a coarse grid — here resampled to 1°, roughly 100 km per cell. It captures the large-scale flow: where the highs and lows are, the broad temperature pattern. HRRR is a regional model over the continental US at 3 km — sharp enough to see terrain-driven winds, convective storms, lake effects. The downscaler learns the map ERA5(coarse) → HRRR(fine) at the same instant in time: given the blurry field, produce a plausible crisp one.

An honest caveat baked in

ERA5 and HRRR come from different models, so this is cross-dataset downscaling, not clean interpolation-inversion. The network will absorb a systematic ERA5↔HRRR bias along with the genuine fine-scale detail. That's intended and useful — but it's a property of the data, worth remembering when you read the outputs.

Why diffusion instead of a plain regression?

A network trained to minimize pixel-wise error learns to hedge: when several sharp outcomes are equally plausible, the safest low-error guess is their blur. That's exactly wrong for extremes — the storms and gusts you most want to downscale. A diffusion model samples instead of averaging, so it commits to one sharp, physically plausible field per draw — and you can draw many to get an ensemble.

02

The big idea: denoising, steered by the coarse field

Diffusion trains a network to remove noise. Do that well enough, at every noise level, and you can start from pure static and walk backwards into a clean field.

Take a real HRRR field and corrupt it: x_noisy = x + σ · noise, where σ (sigma) is how much Gaussian noise you add. The network Dθ is asked to recover the clean x from x_noisy. Crucially it also gets the coarse ERA5 field as a conditioning input, so it never denoises blind — it fills in detail that stays consistent with the large-scale flow.

Drag the slider below through σ. At high σ the field is buried in static; as σ falls, the weather emerges. Training visits all these levels; sampling sweeps them high→low.

03

The denoiser is a U-Net with a transformer bottleneck

A classic encoder–decoder U-Net built from convolutions, with a stack of windowed-attention transformer blocks doing the reasoning at the bottleneck.

It reads the stacked input (noisy target + conditioning), pushes it down through three resolution levels — halving height and width, doubling channels at each — does the heavy reasoning in a small bottleneck, then climbs back up, re-injecting detail through skip connections from the matching encoder level. Only lightweight convolutions run at full 1059×1799 resolution; everything expensive happens at 2×, 4×, and 8× downsampled scales.

Inside a block

Each ResBlock is GroupNorm → SiLU → Conv, twice, with the residual added back — and the noise level injected as a learned embedding so the block knows how noisy its input is. Downsampling uses strided convolutions; the network reflect-pads the odd 1059×1799 grid up to a multiple of 8 so it divides cleanly, then crops back at the end.

The transformer at the bottleneck

Between the two mid ResBlocks sit attn_depth = 4 Swin-style transformer blocks: multi-head self-attention inside 16×16 windows (alternating unshifted/shifted so information crosses window boundaries) plus an MLP, each noise-conditioned via adaLN-Zero — the gates start at zero, so every block is an exact identity at init and training begins from pure-conv behavior. At the ÷8 bottleneck (~133×225) this attention is cheap; guard it off with use_attention = false to recover the pure-conv U-Net.

04

EDM preconditioning: the wrapper that makes it trainable

A raw network struggles to denoise across a range of σ spanning five orders of magnitude. EDM (Karras et al. 2022) fixes this with a thin algebraic wrapper.

Instead of asking the U-Net Fθ to output the clean field directly, the model blends the network's guess with the noisy input using σ-dependent coefficients. The full denoiser is:

Dθ(x, σ, cond) = c_skip(σ)·x + c_out(σ)·Fθ(c_in(σ)·x, c_noise(σ), cond)
coefficientvaluewhat it does
c_skipσ_d² / (σ²+σ_d²) How much to just trust the noisy input. Near 1 at low σ (input is almost clean), near 0 at high σ.
c_outσ·σ_d / √(σ²+σ_d²) How much to trust the network's correction. Grows with σ — at high noise the network does the work.
c_in1 / √(σ²+σ_d²) Rescales the input to ~unit variance before the U-Net sees it, so the net always works at one scale.
c_noiseln(σ) / 4 A tame, log-scaled σ fed to the noise embedding — the block's "how noisy am I" signal.

The payoff: at low σ the model leans on the input and nudges; at high σ it leans on the network and largely invents. With σ_data = 1 — valid because targets are z-scored to unit variance — the algebra stays clean and the network only ever operates on well-scaled inputs.

05

Channels & data: how ERA5 becomes an input

The model is convolutional, so every input must live on the same HRRR grid, as stacked channels. Getting ERA5 there is the plumbing.

  1. Regrid. Each coarse ERA5 driver is bilinearly interpolated from its 1° lat/lon grid onto the HRRR 1059×1799 Lambert-conformal grid. (scipy RegularGridInterpolator)
  2. Normalize. Every channel is z-scored with precomputed per-channel mean and std, so all inputs are comparable in scale.
  3. Add statics. Two constant maps — orog (terrain height) and lsm (land–sea mask) — give the network geography to hang detail on.
  4. Stack the target. The HRRR field being denoised (its own noisy copy) rides in the same tensor as extra channels.
A meaningful split: prognostic vs diagnostic

Prognostic fields (temperature, winds, surface pressure) get cos-latitude spatial weighting in the loss. The diagnostic field (precipitation, in log space) is heavy-tailed and treated separately, unweighted, so a few extreme cells don't dominate.

06

The training loop, one step at a time

Every step is the same wager: corrupt a real HRRR field, ask the model to undo it, and score how close it got.

  1. Sample a noise level. ln σ ~ 𝒩(−1.2, 1.2²), clamped to [0.002, 80]. A log-normal draw means the model trains on mostly moderate noise but sees the extremes too.
  2. Corrupt. x_noisy = target + σ·noise, with the coarse ERA5 conditioning left untouched.
  3. Denoise. Run Dθ(x_noisy, σ, cond) — the EDM-wrapped U-Net.
  4. Score. A σ-weighted MSE: λ(σ) = (σ²+σ_d²)/(σ·σ_d)² upweights the levels where learning is hardest, then cos-lat and per-variable weights shape it further.
  5. Update. AdamW with a cosine LR schedule; gradients clipped; bf16 mixed precision; accumulated across micro-batches and all-reduced across GPUs (DDP).
  6. Track a shadow. An EMA of the weights (decay 0.9999) is what you actually sample from — smoother and more stable than the raw training weights.
Why weight by σ at all

Without λ(σ), the tiny errors at low noise and huge errors at high noise would be scored on wildly different scales, and the model would only bother learning one regime. The weighting equalizes them so every noise level pulls its weight.

07

Sampling: from static to a downscaled field

Inference runs the noise schedule backwards, and — unlike a forecast rollout — it happens in one shot.

Start from pure Gaussian noise scaled by σ_max = 80. Then follow a fixed schedule down to 0 (Karras' ρ = 7 spacing, ~18 steps) using a Heun 2nd-order ODE solver — each step predicts, takes an Euler step, then corrects. At every step the same ERA5 conditioning steers the denoiser, so the emerging field stays faithful to the coarse flow. The result is one downscaled HRRR field.

Want uncertainty? Draw several samples with a little stochastic churn — each is a different, equally-plausible fine field consistent with the same coarse input. That's your ensemble.

One shot, not a rollout

The model this architecture was ported from is a forecast emulator: it steps HRRR forward 6 hours at a time, feeding each output back in. A downscaler has no such loop — there's nothing to roll forward. You condition on ERA5 at time T and sample HRRR at time T, done. Reusing the emulator's autoregressive sampler here would be a category error, which is why inference was rebuilt as single-shot generation.

08

Emulator → downscaler: what actually changed

The architecture was lifted verbatim from an HRRR forecast emulator. Every difference lives in the data and the task framing — not the network.

That's the elegance of the port: denoising is task-agnostic. Swap what goes in and what comes out, and the same generative core learns a completely different job.

Glossary

σ (sigma)
The noise level — standard deviation of the Gaussian noise added to a field. Training spans 0.002 → 80.
EDM
Elucidated Diffusion Model (Karras et al. 2022) — the preconditioning + noise-schedule recipe used here.
ERA5
Global reanalysis; the coarse 1° input driving the downscaler.
HRRR
High-Resolution Rapid Refresh; the fine 3 km CONUS target.
Prognostic / diagnostic
Core state variables (temp, wind, pressure) vs derived heavy-tailed fields (precipitation).
Conditioning
Extra input channels the denoiser is guided by — here, the coarse ERA5 field — so it doesn't invent freely.
cos-lat weighting
Weighting the loss by cosine of latitude so grid cells count by true area, not pixel count.
EMA
Exponential moving average of the weights; the smoothed copy used for sampling and evaluation.
Heun solver
A 2nd-order predictor–corrector ODE step; turns the trained denoiser into a sampler in ~18 steps.
CONUS · Lambert
Continental US, on HRRR's Lambert-conformal 1059×1799 grid.
